Finding 1: Education and home values drive lending and business density — race is tangled up in both

The strongest predictor of CRA lending per capita is median home value (+0.80), followed by education (+0.68). The same two factors also predict business density (+0.71 and +0.69). But education and income are not evenly distributed by race: Asian residents hold college degrees at rates of 62–92% and earn $77K–$108K, while Hispanic residents range from 29–65% and earn $28K–$56K. The racial earnings gap is widest in the counties that receive the most lending. In Essex, where CRA lending is high, Asian workers earn $101K vs. Hispanic workers at $36K. The data can’t tell us whether lending follows wealth or reinforces it, but the pattern is clear: the communities that get the most capital are the ones where racial economic gaps are largest.

Finding 2: Immigrant communities start businesses faster — but specific origins matter

Counties with larger Ecuadorian-born (+0.53), South American (+0.53), Caribbean (+0.52), and Filipino (+0.50) populations have significantly higher rates of new business formation. This pattern holds even after adjusting for income. But not all immigrant groups show the same signal: Korean and Indian communities don’t predict entrepreneurship — they predict employment concentration (+0.76 for Chinese-born, +0.70 for Indian-born jobs per capita). These communities cluster near corporate employers rather than starting small businesses. Mexican-born residents show no entrepreneurship relationship at all. The takeaway: “immigrant entrepreneurship” is not a single story — different origin communities relate to the economy in fundamentally different ways.

Finding 3: CRA and SBA lending flow to different communities for different reasons

CRA lending tracks property values (+0.80) and European-born share (+0.66) — it goes where real estate is most valuable. SBA lending tracks education (+0.62) and income (+0.60), and SBA dollars per capita correlate with White non-Hispanic share (+0.46). These are two parallel capital pipelines serving different parts of the economy. CRA supports residential real estate in high-value markets. SBA supports small business in established, higher-income communities. Counties with high immigrant entrepreneurship rates (Hudson, Passaic, Essex) often receive less SBA capital per capita than their business formation rates would suggest — a potential gap between where businesses are starting and where federal small-business capital flows.

Finding 4: Net job creation and construction have no demographic predictors

Despite strong links between demographics and lending, business density, and employment concentration, two outcomes are completely independent of community characteristics. Net job creation shows no relationship with any demographic variable (all |r| < 0.08) — jobs come and go based on economic cycles and industry decisions. New housing permits are similarly demographic-free (all |r| < 0.22) — construction follows zoning, land availability, and market timing. This means the demographic patterns that shape lending and business formation do not extend to job growth or housing supply. Those are structurally determined.

Finding 5: Education is the floor for everything — Cumberland proves it

Cumberland County has a 35% Hispanic population and 12% foreign-born share, but the lowest entrepreneurship rate (8.7%) and fewest businesses per capita in the state. It also has the lowest college-degree rate (18%) and lowest median income ($64K). Without a baseline of education and economic infrastructure, immigrant entrepreneurship doesn’t activate. Compare with Hudson County: 43% foreign-born, 48% college-educated, and the highest entrepreneurship rate (12.1%). The same immigrant energy produces different outcomes depending on the educational and economic foundation underneath it. Ocean County presents the opposite puzzle: low immigration (8%) but high entrepreneurship (12.0%), driven by a booming housing market and retiree economy rather than immigrant business formation.

Finding 6: % Black population inversely tracks lending, businesses, and employment

Counties with larger Black populations receive less CRA lending per capita (-0.40), have fewer businesses per person (-0.40), and receive less SBA capital. This mirrors the education-income pattern: Black college completion rates (33–70%) and earnings ($28K–$58K) trail other groups in most counties. But the relationship is not simply “demographics cause outcomes.” The same counties with large Black populations also have lower home values, lower overall education rates, and higher poverty — the factors that independently drive lending and business formation. Disentangling race from the structural economic conditions it correlates with is the central challenge these data present.

County patterns play out differently at the municipal level

County-level data reveals broad relationships between demographics and economic outcomes, but NJ’s 565 municipalities vary enormously within each county. A single county can contain both a major city and rural farmland. Outliers at the municipal level

Lakewood Township (Ocean County) dominates: $1.96 billion in CRA lending, 4,301 new housing units, and $967M in construction — more than most entire counties. This one municipality largely explains Ocean County’s outlier status in the county-level data (high lending despite low immigrant share).

Jersey City (Hudson County) leads in housing permits (29,163 units) and construction ($7.4 billion), reflecting massive urban redevelopment. But it ranks 4th in CRA lending volume — a gap that suggests development capital flows through channels other than CRA.

North Caldwell Borough (Essex County) is a surprise #2 in CRA lending ($1.62 billion) despite being a small, wealthy suburb with only 145 new housing units — suggesting very high per-unit lending values in affluent communities.

Equity Insight: The Pattern Behind the Numbers

Home values and education are the strongest predictors of CRA lending. This isn't necessarily unfair lending — it reflects where property values are highest and households have resources to invest. The negative relationship with Black residents reflects demographic sorting: in the wealthiest NJ counties, Black residents are concentrated in specific, often older areas. CRA lending follows capital, and capital has already concentrated in certain neighborhoods. Breaking this pattern requires building opportunity in undervalued communities, not changing lending decisions.

Cumberland County Anomaly

Cumberland County has a high Hispanic population (primarily Puerto Rican and Dominican) but the lowest entrepreneurship rate in NJ. Why? Education levels are the floor. Despite high immigration, limited college attainment constrains business formation. This shows that immigration patterns alone don't drive entrepreneurship — local economic opportunity and skills matter more.

Ocean County Outlier

Ocean County has low foreign-born population but high entrepreneurship rates. The driver: a housing boom that created construction and real estate opportunities. This shows entrepreneurship follows economic cycles and available niches, not just immigrant presence.

Two Paths for Immigrant Communities

Path 1 (Entrepreneurship): Ecuadorian, South American, Caribbean, and Filipino communities show strong business formation. These groups are creating new enterprises. Path 2 (Employment Clustering): Chinese, Korean, and Indian communities cluster in high-employment areas (tech hubs, corporate centers). They're filling jobs rather than creating them. Both paths represent economic mobility and participation — they're just different roles in the economy.

Key Finding: Demographics Don't Predict Housing Construction

Housing construction is driven by zoning, land availability, and real estate market cycles — not by racial or ethnic composition. We tested all demographic variables and found no strong relationships (all |r| < 0.22 with new housing permits). A county gets new housing because land is available and the market is hot, not because of who lives there.

Construction vs Lending vs Business Formation

How Are These Connected?

Construction, lending, and entrepreneurship are three separate economic streams in NJ counties. Construction depends on real estate cycles and zoning. Lending follows property values and education levels. Business formation depends partly on entrepreneurial communities and partly on local opportunity (like the Ocean County housing boom). They're related but not determined by the same forces.
