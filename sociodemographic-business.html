Three distinct stories in the data

1. Where businesses cluster is about wealth and education. Counties with higher home values, more college graduates, and higher incomes have more businesses per person.

2. Where new businesses start fastest is a different story. Counties with larger immigrant populations create new businesses at a higher rate — even after accounting for income differences. This pattern has been consistent every year from 2015 to 2023.

3. Net job creation has no connection to any community characteristic we tested. Jobs come and go based on the economy, not demographics.

How to read these charts

A quick guide to what “relationship strength” means

Strong

The two things move closely together. If you know one, you can make a good guess at the other. Counties high on one tend to be high (or low) on the other.

Moderate

There’s a real pattern, but plenty of exceptions. The connection is meaningful but other factors clearly matter too.

Weak / None

Little to no pattern. Knowing one tells you almost nothing about the other. The two things vary independently.

Education and home values matter most

Counties where more residents hold college degrees and where home values are higher consistently have more businesses per person. This likely reflects a combination of factors: educated populations both start and attract businesses, while high property values signal economic vitality and purchasing power. Income and low poverty/unemployment follow the same pattern but are somewhat weaker predictors.

Immigrant communities create new businesses at a faster pace

Counties with more foreign-born residents have a higher entrepreneurship rate — new businesses as a percentage of existing ones. This isn’t because these counties are wealthier (the pattern holds even after accounting for income) and it isn’t a one-time fluke (it has shown up every single year from 2015 to 2023).

Interestingly, the high-immigrant counties that lead in entrepreneurship rate — Hudson, Essex, Passaic, Middlesex — don’t necessarily have the most businesses per person. They have fewer established businesses but create new ones faster. The churn is higher.

Notable outliers

Ocean County breaks the immigrant pattern — very low immigrant share (8%) but the 2nd-highest entrepreneurship rate (12.0%). Ocean’s high birth rate likely reflects its booming housing market and retiree economy driving small business formation.

Cumberland County has a high Hispanic population (35%) but the lowest entrepreneurship rate (8.7%). It also has the lowest education levels (18% with college degrees) and lowest incomes in the state, suggesting that immigrant entrepreneurship requires a minimum threshold of economic infrastructure.

Net job creation has no demographic pattern

We tested whether income, education, race, immigration status, homeownership, or any other community characteristic predicts net job gains or losses. None of them do. Every relationship we tested was essentially random (all very weak). Job creation is driven by economic cycles, industry mix, and individual business decisions — not by who lives in a county.

But: Where Jobs Exist Is Predictable

Employment concentration (jobs per capita) is strongly linked to demographics

Where jobs exist vs. where jobs are created — two different questions

While we can’t predict changes in employment, the level of employment concentration is highly predictable. Counties with larger Asian populations and more college graduates have far more jobs per capita. This reflects where large employers — corporate offices, hospitals, universities — choose to locate, not where jobs are being added year-over-year.
